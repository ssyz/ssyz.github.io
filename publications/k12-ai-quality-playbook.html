In the last six months, AI quality in K-12 has shifted from a reputation issue to a procurement one. Districts are building AI requirements into RFPs, states are putting out guidance, and boards are asking questions product teams can’t just leave to marketing anymore.

If you’re reading this, you already feel that pressure. The question isn’t whether to evaluate, but how to do it in a way your team can actually sustain. This playbook is a 3-step loop for getting there: Measure, Diagnose, Build. It’s the framework we use with vendor teams who need defensible quality evidence on a product-team timeline.

Why This Playbook Exists

The Credibility Gap Is Two-Sided

~10%

of AI-based approaches in education have undergone rigorous independent validation.UNESCO

The other 90% are shipping anyway, which puts both sides of the market in a difficult spot.

Buyers are asked to trust AI tools without independent evidence. Sellers lack standardized methods for proving quality — not because they don’t want to, but because deep AI evaluation expertise sits outside most product teams’ core capabilities. This is a structural gap in the toolchain. This playbook seeks to close that gap with a repeatable loop vendors can adopt.

What This Is (and What It Isn’t)

What it is: a 3-step loop framed for vendor product and engineering teams, with concrete deliverables at each step and a path that scales from a one-time experiment to a permanent quality function.

What it isn’t:

  • Not an academic methodology paper.
  • Not a model-selection guide. The loop applies regardless of which foundation model you ship on.

Who this is for: product and engineering teams shipping AI features that generate educational content — lessons, passages, items, feedback, tutoring turns. If your AI surface is non-content (recommenders, analytics, scheduling), the loop’s shape still applies but the rubrics in Step 1 won’t.

The Independent Evaluation Landscape

Before we get to the loop, it’s worth knowing what the landscape actually looks like. A vendor exploring “third-party eval” today has four real options:

Source Strength Weakness
Internal evals(your own QA) Cheap, fast, deeply contextual Not independent — can’t be cited externally
Horizontal platforms(Braintrust, LangSmith, Galileo) Strong tooling, broad coverage Generic; no K-12 domain rubrics or established frameworks
Nonprofits(ISTE, Common Sense) Trusted brand in education Rubric-based reviews of features, not measurement of content quality; slower cycles
Academic / research(WestEd, SRI) Gold-standard rigor $150K+ engagements, 12–36 months, RCT-shaped

Each of these has a place. Internal evals are how you ship daily, and horizontal platforms are where most teams pick up tooling. Nonprofits and academics carry weight in policy contexts.

Nevertheless, the market has a missing quadrant: fast, independent, and K-12-deep — content-quality measurement against established frameworks (Student Achievement Partners, CAST, ANet), on a timeline a product team can actually use against the next release. That’s the gap this playbook is written from.

The Quality Loop

The loop has three steps, each answering a different question:

  • Measure — Is my AI good enough?
  • Diagnose — Where and why is it failing?
  • Build — How do I prevent regression and ship faster?

The steps map to a vendor’s quality maturity. Measure produces one-off evidence, like a snapshot you can put in a sales deck or RFP response. Diagnose turns that snapshot into a roadmap. Build turns the roadmap into infrastructure: continuous quality assurance that holds up across model swaps, prompt changes, and new content domains.

Watch out

Three ways vendors misimplement the loop

  1. The one-shot trap. A single eval result becomes a marketing asset and the loop stops. The next model update silently regresses; nobody notices until a district complains.
  2. The build-without-diagnose trap. A team buys observability tooling, instruments everything, and ends up with dashboards full of metrics that don’t tell anyone what to fix. Diagnosis is the bridge between measurement and action; without it, “Build” becomes vanity infrastructure.
  3. The self-evaluation trap. Internal QA stands in for independent evaluation. This works until a district asks for evidence — at which point the vendor has rich internal data and nothing citable.
01 Step One

Measure AI Content Quality

Good measurement in K-12 has three properties: it’s grounded in established frameworks (Student Achievement Partners, CAST, Achievement Network), it uses domain-specific rubrics, and it evaluates actual AI output — not feature checklists. A grade-level accuracy score on actual generated passages tells you something a yes/no on “supports differentiation” never will.

Why third-party? Three reasons stack: procurement credibility (districts increasingly require it), marketing defensibility (independent numbers travel further than internal ones), and board-level pressure (your CEO is being asked questions that need an answer with a citation).

A defensible third-party measurement engagement typically lands in the low thousands of dollars and weeks-not-months. That’s worth saying out loud because most vendors assume “third-party” means six-figure academic study. It doesn’t have to. Internal-only QA costs nothing on paper but it doesn’t build trust with external stakeholders, and trust is paramount for product success.

Measurement also has to actually discriminate between tools. In our first vendor benchmark, Twee scored 89% on grade-level accuracy in Science while MagicSchool’s February evaluation put them at 35% on the same dimension. That spread is the whole point of measurement — it surfaces the differences a buyer needs to make a decision and gives a vendor a defensible number to point at.

02 Step Two

Diagnose Failure Modes

Measurement tells you whether you have a problem. Diagnosis tells you where and why — and that’s what makes the next sprint possible.

The MagicSchool February evaluation is a good worked example. The aggregate score (66%) was useful for a press release, but the failure-mode heatmap was useful for engineering. Failures localized hard: Science was the weakest subject, “Life Cycles & Traits” was the weakest unit within Science, and every failure across the dataset skewed too hard. That’s three actionable findings from one evaluation, and each implies a different fix.

A taxonomy of common K-12 AI failure modes that vendors should expect to find:

  • Grade-level drift — content reads above or below the target band. Skews have different fixes. For example, too-hard usually means vocabulary or syntactic complexity; too-easy usually means the model is hedging away from substance.
  • Subject-specific weakness — Science is consistently harder than Social Studies for current models. Our Benchmark Note #2 found a 12.4pp gap on grade-level accuracy across frontier models. Technical vocabulary is hard to simplify without distorting meaning.
  • Specialized-vocabulary blind spots — domain terms that read fine to an adult but land above grade level for the target reader. Often hides inside otherwise well-leveled prose.
  • Judge disagreement — when your evaluation judges disagree with each other, that’s not noise to average away. It’s a signal of model uncertainty, often pointing at content the underlying generator is least confident about.

Diagnosis is the bridge between measurement and action. Without it, a measurement result is just a number. With it, you have a prioritized list of where to point the next sprint.

03 Step Three

Build the System

A single evaluation gives you a snapshot. An eval system unlocks a continuous improvement flywheel.

The clearest illustration is the MagicSchool February-to-March arc. One diagnosis cycle, one sprint of changes, and the headline numbers moved from 66% to 71% overall — with Science specifically jumping from 35% to 52%. The same loop, run twice, produced a measurable, defensible improvement. That’s what continuous quality looks like in practice.

Why iteration matters: AI quality isn’t a launch problem, it’s an operational one. An inherent characteristic of LLM-based applications is that models drift and prompts decay. New content domains break things that worked last quarter. One-shot evaluation gives you a snapshot, while continuous evaluation tells you when the snapshot is no longer true.

Your Eval System in Practice

Imagine you’ve just shipped a passage-generation feature. You run a baseline — 200 passages graded against the rubric, evaluated by multiple judges so the score isn’t an artifact of any one model’s quirks. The result: 78% overall, but only 54% on STEM, with every failure skewing too hard. That’s your Measure step, and it’s already handed you something specific to work on.

Diagnosis points at a prompt that constrains vocabulary, but not syntactic complexity in technical content. You add a sentence-length cap and tell the model to introduce a concrete example before any abstract concept. That’s Diagnose — not a deck, just a localized hypothesis your engineers can act on this sprint. Re-run the suite: STEM jumps to 71%, overall to 82%. You ship.

So far, this is a one-shot. Where it becomes a system is what happens next. You wire that 200-passage suite into CI with thresholds that flag any subject-level regression. Six weeks later, a foundation-model upgrade silently drops STEM back to 60%. The suite catches it within a day. You roll back, adapt the prompts through offline experimentation, and ship the upgrade two weeks later without a customer ever seeing the regression. That’s the Build step paying off.

The benefit is operational confidence. The team running the loop on every release is the team that doesn’t get blindsided and the team with receipts when a district asks how AI quality is monitored.

Compounding Returns

One eval gives you a snapshot. An eval system is critical quality infrastructure that benefits multiple audiences:

  • Product knows what to build next. Sprint planning starts from a ranked list of measured failures, not the loudest customer complaint.
  • Marketing and sales have independent numbers to point at. RFP responses stop being prose-heavy and start being citation-heavy.
  • Procurement-facing teams have answers ready when district questions land. The cost of a procurement cycle drops.

There’s a quieter compounding effect, too. Failure-mode taxonomies accumulate. Each evaluation a vendor runs against a shared methodology contributes to a growing K-12 benchmark dataset, which means each new diagnosis is informed by patterns observed across the ecosystem. The third vendor to evaluate Science content gets a sharper diagnosis than the first, because the first two have come before. Vendors who participate in standardized benchmarking inherit that compounding sharpness; those who don’t, run their loops in isolation.

Appendix: Drop-in RFP Language

Districts increasingly include AI-quality questions in RFPs, and proposal teams need defensible answers fast. The paragraphs below are ready to use — just adapt the names and link targets to your own product.

RFP Question

“How do you validate the quality of AI-generated content?”

We validate AI-generated content through independent third-party evaluation against established educational frameworks, including those developed by Student Achievement Partners, CAST, and Achievement Network. Our content is benchmarked using OurDojo’s published K-12 GenAI evaluation methodology, which evaluates real generated output against domain-specific rubrics rather than feature checklists. Independent benchmark results are available at [link to OurDojo evaluation].

RFP Question

“How do you monitor AI quality over time?”

Quality monitoring is treated as an operational, not one-time, function. We run a regression evaluation suite on every model or prompt change, and we re-baseline against the OurDojo benchmark methodology at a regular cadence. Our most recent iteration cycle moved [headline metric] from X% to Y% — see [link to iterative case study] for an illustration of the process.
